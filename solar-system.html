Solar System

Significant discoveries made on our Solar System

  • 16th Century

    Helio-Centric Model of the Solar System

    The Geocentric model initially suggested that the Earth occupied the central position in our solar system, with all other planets orbiting around it. Despite Aristarchus suggesting the Sun as the central body, the Geocentric model remained for centuries. Ptolemy enhanced this model by introducing the concept of epicycles, suggesting that planets followed smaller circular paths, themselves revolving around the larger circle representing the planet's orbit. It wasn't until Copernicus proposed a heliocentric model, positioning the Sun at the center, with Earth and other planets in orbit around it.

  • Early 17th century (1610)

    Galileo Moons

    Galileo Galilei made groundbreaking observations of Jupiter, revealing its four largest moons, commonly referred to as the Galilean moons. These moons, Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto. Each moon possesses distinct characteristics, Io is known for its volcanic activity, Europa has an icy surface with a potential subsurface ocean, Ganymede is the largest moon with a magnetic field and Callisto reveals a heavily cratered surface

  • 16th Century

    Laws of Planetary Motion

    Kepler's laws describe planetary motion, explained through a set of three principles. The First law states that planets move in elliptical orbits with the sun at one of the foci. Kepler's Second Law, also known as the Law of Equal Areas, says a planet moves faster when it's closer to the sun in its orbit. This is because, in the same amount of time, it covers more space around the sun. Lastly, the Third Law relates to the time it takes for planets to complete an orbit around the sun based on their elliptical paths, where greater distances of planets from the Sun result in longer orbital periods.

  • September 1977

    Voyager 1

    Voyager 1 holds the distinction of being the first human-made spacecraft launched into space. Its primary mission aimed to observe the outer planets, specifically Jupiter and Saturn. In 1979, Voyager 1 executed a close encounter with Jupiter, capturing detailed images of its moons as well as the volcanic activity on Io. During its meeting with Saturn, the spacecraft delivered remarkable images of Saturn's rings, revealing details of the tiny cloud particles surrounding Saturn. The historic encounter with Saturn occurred at a proximity of 78,000 miles.
